# Reduced batch size

## Twitter

I have another NanoGPT Speedrun record at 2.936 mins!

This time around, I encountered some strange phenomenon: fp8 scales can not only affect validation loss, but also the wallclock time

But before that, let's talk about the more scalable changes first:

1. Reduced training sequence length (from 64*1024 to 48*1024). This was inspired by the "critical batch size" literature. The gist is:

- The larger your batch size/seq length is, the more information you can gather on what the "true" gradients should be. This leads to less "noisy" gradients during training, and thus faster convergence.

- However, there are diminishing returns on this: at some point, the extra cost per-step isn't worth the gradient noise reduction anymore. And you're better off with a smaller batch size/seq length in exchange for more training steps.

- But as we decrease the seq length and thus increase the number of training steps, we encounter another problem: there is an irreducible per-step overhead, perhaps coming up GPU-to-GPU comms, etc. And the more you increase the training steps, the larger this irreducible overhead would be.

Thus, there's an "optimal sequence length". And after a bit of binary-searching, it turns out to be around 48*1024

2. Increased validation sequence length (from 64*1024 to 256*1024). This was actually Keller's idea. 

Interestingly, even without rope-scaling, this helped improve the validation loss by 0.0015, allowing us to reduce training steps by 10 (or around 700 ms to 1 sec improvement).

This serves as further evidence that the Long-Short Sliding-Window Attention we developed for the speedrun indeed generalizes to sequence lengths beyond what we trained it on.

And finally,

3. Improved fp8 scales for the LM head.

For context, we don't dynamically update the fp8 scales because (1) they're relatively stable throughout training anyway and (2) doing so would incur overhead. And so, we just used a fixed set of fp8 scales.

And when I updated the sequence length, I also had to update the fp8 scales. I traced the max-absolute value of the elements in the weights & gradients, and used the following formula to get the new scales:

- new_weight_scale = 2 ** int(log_2(0.8 * max_fp8_e4m3 / weight_max_abs))
- new_grad_scale = 2 ** int(log_2(0.8 * max_fp8_e5m2 / grad_max_abs))

And here's where it gets weird: I thought it was only gonna improve the validation loss. But apparently, it also reduced wallclock time.

What the fuck is going on?

Let's focus on the grad scales because it had the most effect. The new scale is 2**19 while the old scale is 2**29

And another thing to note is that the distribution of entries in gradient matrices usually follow a power-law-ish. I.e., most entries are clustered around zero while some are exponentially higher than the rest.

So, the old grad scale, being higher than it should be, causes a fraction of the entries to be clamped at inf. While the new grad scale, being lower than it should be for some iterations, causes a much larger fraction of entries to be 0.

So, our new scale is actually zeroing out a lot of entries in our grad matrices. And remember Horace's blog post on how it's actually faster to do matrix multiplications on more predictable matrices? I think that's what's going on here.

What does this mean for large-scale, planet-wide distributed LLM training? That we don't actually need to send the entire gradient when doing all-reduces. It's fine to zero-out the small entries and just don't send them.
