First is Long-Short Sliding Window Attention

This attention mechanism is inspired by the Global-Local attention in Gemma 2 (and more recent "hybrid" architectures. However, there are two key differences:

1. Instead of mixing full attention with linear attention layers, we instead use Sliding Window Attention with some layers having double the context length ("long SWA") than others ("short SWA") at any given training step.

2. We also warmup the context length of the SWA at different rates. I.e., we increase the context length of the long SWAs at double the rate than the short SWAs as training progresses.

This helped us shave off 3 ms/step. And, after compensating the increase in val_loss by adding 15 training steps, we were able to shave off ~2-3 secs* overall.

*it's actually a 3.2 secs improvement by itself (https://github.com/leloykun/modded-nanogpt/tree/fc--global-local-attn-2/records/011525_GlobalLocalAttn). But effects became smaller as we added more optimizations & further tightened the speedrun.

Next is Increased Attention Scale

1.0/sqrt(head_dim)=0.88 -> 0.12

You might find this strange--and indeed, this is a speedrun specific optimization more than anything. But we do have a theory on why this helps...

---

So, we currently use QK-Normalization to stabilize the attention coefficients. This helped reduce training steps & wallclock time a few weeks back (see https://x.com/kellerjordan0/status/1845865698532450646). However, unlike in larger-scale models such as ViT-22B and Chameleon which uses LayerNorm with learnable parameters as usual, we instead use a parameter-free RMSNorm. It's faster and leads to more stable training runs (for the speedrun, at least).

The problem with using parameter-free RMSNorms for doing QK-Normalization, however, is that it limits the sharpness of the logits--inducing a lower bound on the attention entropy. This makes the model less "certain" which tokens to "attend to" during training.

While not problematic early in training as we don't want the model to overfit quickly, it can be problematic later on when we want the model to "focus" on the most important tokens. And the current record is now tight-enough for this to be a problem.

Stacked QKV Weights & Batched Muon Implementation

This is a compiler-aware optimization that leads to a 1-2 secs speed improvement. The crux is that, with a big enough GPU, doing one massive matmul for the QKV weights is faster than doing three smaller matmuls, one for each of the weights.

The problem, however, is that Muon performs better on the unmerged QKV weights primarily due to the massive matmuls in its Newton-Schulz iterations. 

The implementation in the previous record attempt involved storing these weights separately as before but concatting them in the forward pass. However, this concat introduced a ~1 sec regression which somewhat, but not entirely, cancelled out its gains.

@YouJiacheng
 then pointed out that we can get rid of this overhead by using the batched implementation of Muon by 
@scottjmaddox
!

Combined, this allowed us to reduce wallclock time by ~1-2 secs.


And finally, the Adam `eps=1e-10` fix

https://x.com/hi_tysam/status/1879692937589875094

I believe the primary reason for the 'bug' reported here is that we initialize the LM head as a zero matrix. This leads to small gradients early on which could sometimes be even smaller than Adam's default epsilon--causing training instability and increased validation loss.

Simply setting Adam's epsilson to 1e-10 as suggested by 
@YouJiacheng
 allowed us to reduce training steps by 10 (or a ~1 sec improvement)
